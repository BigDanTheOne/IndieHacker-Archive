# Hitting a $1.6M ARR plateau but refusing VC funding

**Author:** James Fleischmann
**Date:** July 3, 2025
**Source:** https://www.indiehackers.com/post/a00nu8BecGGRVXNdbT4j

---

Christopher Gimmer bootstrapped Snappa--a graphic design tool for marketers--without venture capital. The platform reached $1.6M ARR through content marketing and SEO before plateauing around 2021. Prior to Snappa, Gimmer built StockSnap (a stock photo site) and BootstrapBay (a marketplace).

Gimmer's approach to growth centered on customer discovery: surveying 200+ StockSnap users via welcome email and conducting 15-20 customer interviews. He created a content flywheel with high-intent blog posts ("Facebook cover photo size"), landing pages for specific use cases, and templates--creating momentum through interconnected content. He also leveraged existing audiences from earlier projects.

A data-driven approach tracked signups and conversions by landing page source to optimize focus. Growth eventually plateaued due to limited scalable marketing channels beyond SEO, increased competition from well-funded competitors (notably Canva), and a market shift toward video content that reduced demand for static graphics tools.

Gimmer practiced "stair stepping"--building progressively ambitious projects rather than launching a full SaaS immediately. He started small with affiliate sites, moved to StockSnap and BootstrapBay, and then tackled Snappa.

The team remains lean at 4 people: Gimmer, cofounder Marc, and 2 full-time employees. Rather than pursue VC funding or force growth through video pivots, Gimmer accepted the plateau and launched GoodMetrics--a privacy-first Google Analytics alternative--targeting $1M+ ARR. He optimizes for operational freedom over maximum revenue extraction.

**Revenue Numbers:**
- Snappa peak ARR: $1.6M
- Current Snappa revenue: below $1M ARR (declined from peak)
- Team size: 4 people

**Key Lessons:**
- "Start small. Build calm. Scale with intention." -- stair-stepping approach builds skills progressively
- Distribution matters equally to product quality: "A great product is only half the battle--if no one knows about it, it doesn't matter"
- "Ship something useful, listen to users, and keep improving it. That feedback loop is way more valuable than trying to nail everything upfront"
- Be proactive about evolving the product or exploring adjacent opportunities before growth stalls
- Content flywheel: high-intent blog posts, landing pages, and templates create compounding growth

**Tech Stack:**
PHP, JavaScript (original), Node.js (current), Amazon AWS, DigitalOcean
