# The definitive product positioning framework (part one)

**Author:** Anthony Pierri
**Date:** December 13, 2024
**Source:** https://www.indiehackers.com/post/lBlFQinQscH27s5VdrXr

---

The author distinguishes between product positioning (who to target + how to differentiate) and brand positioning (vision, purpose, emotional connections). Positioning represents "a strategic bet, not a quickly testable hypothesis" -- meaning it cannot be validated through A/B testing like messaging can.

**Critical Framework Elements:**

1. **Use Case-Based Segmentation** is superior to firmographic segmentation because it identifies actual workflows users need to perform, not just company characteristics.

2. **Use Case Maturity Spectrum**: Activities range from immature (no dedicated product category) to mature (established categories like "CRM software"). Examples include Calendly transforming "email meeting scheduling" into a recognized category.

3. **Width of Use Cases**: Broader use cases capture larger markets but create messaging ambiguity; narrower ones enable sharper positioning but smaller addressable markets.

4. **Immature vs. Mature Segments Trade-offs**:
   - Immature: Less competition, longer education cycles
   - Mature: Immediate market understanding, requires genuine differentiation

**Revenue Numbers Mentioned:**

**Key Lessons:**
1. CEOs must own positioning decisions -- delegation undermines strategy execution across departments.
2. Homepages serve as the most accessible positioning asset, preventing "positioning drift" when strategy is locked in public-facing copy.
3. Targeting decisions require subjective judgment; no dataset definitively indicates optimal segment selection.
4. Use case maps and positioning canvases provide structured tools for narrowing segment options systematically.
5. Avoid measuring positioning effectiveness through homepage conversion rates; position strategically, then test messaging variations.
