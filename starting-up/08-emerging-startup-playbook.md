# The Emerging Startup Playbook

**Author:** Kyle Poyar
**Date:** October 25, 2024
**Source:** https://www.indiehackers.com/post/starting-up/the-emerging-startup-playbook-sqDmfZOvr06ZpdMJqYb7

---

Kyle Poyar examines how breakthrough startups have diverged from the conventional playbook that dominated the past decade. Rather than following the traditional path of launching with PR fanfare, aggressive cold outbound, and rapid hiring, emerging companies are adopting eight distinct practices suited to today's post-ZIRP, AI-first environment.

## The Eight Emerging Practices

### 1. Build a Minimum Remarkable Product

The outdated advice to ship embarrassingly early no longer applies. Jam co-founder Dani Grant explained: "Today, quality is non-negotiable." After seven failed launches, Jam's eighth attempt succeeded specifically because "the product was bug-free." When alternatives include other modern software products rather than spreadsheets, first impressions determine whether users return or abandon your product entirely.

### 2. Embrace Storytelling to Attract an Audience

Instead of pursuing lightning-strike launches with PR exclusives, founders are building audiences through authentic, personality-driven content on social platforms and newsletters. Adam Schoenfeld grew his LinkedIn audience from zero to 14,000 in under 18 months through consistent, zero-click B2B content -- without paid ads. This strategy delivered eight pre-launch customers organically.

### 3. Hire Operations to Test and Grow Channels

Rather than defaulting to cold outbound, companies systematically test multiple acquisition channels. Rows ran 10 experiments across four channels in eight weeks before scaling winners. This requires operationally-minded generalists who can move quickly and measure what works.

### 4. Lead With the Product

Instead of SEO-bait content, successful startups use lead magnets, interactive demos, and free tools as marketing. Copy.ai built 30+ AI-powered tools that attracted millions of users. Rows removed their homepage entirely, letting visitors try the product immediately without signup -- tripling conversion rates from 11% to 27%.

### 5. Strong Retention and Word-of-Mouth Signal PMF

The $1 million ARR milestone is less meaningful than it once was. Attio tracked daily-to-monthly active user ratios to measure product stickiness. Jam watched retention streaks week-over-week in a simple spreadsheet. These metrics reveal whether customers genuinely find value or use the product sporadically.

### 6. Stay Lean Through Automation and AI

HeyGen scaled to reported $35 million in ARR while remaining profitable with an exceptionally lean team. Joshua Xu noted: "More people isn't always better. A lean team that can move fast is more important." Folk operates with only one growth person, marketer, and SDR despite 100,000 users and 5x year-over-year growth. AI SDRs and automation replace traditional hiring approaches.

### 7. Embrace a Unified GTM Focused on Ideal Customers

The marketing-qualified lead (MQL) paradigm creates attribution disputes and encourages spray-and-pray tactics. ClickUp's COO emphasized that "revenue machines in B2B are becoming more holistic." Teams should focus on reaching target accounts, understanding where they sit in buying journeys, and measuring which activities drive actual influence -- regardless of channel.

### 8. Maintain Capital Optionality

Outside funding becomes optional rather than essential. Startups following this playbook can bootstrap (lemlist reached $28 million ARR bootstrapped) or raise strategically when opportunities demand acceleration. The emphasis shifts to profitability pathways and founder control over company destiny.

## Key Themes

These practices share common threads: prioritizing durable customer relationships over vanity metrics, building efficiently rather than hiring aggressively, and pursuing sustainable growth. The emerging playbook reflects how software distribution has become harder while quality bars have risen and capital markets have tightened.

Poyar positions these as evolving best practices rather than rigid formulas, acknowledging that successful startups will continue innovating beyond these eight principles.
